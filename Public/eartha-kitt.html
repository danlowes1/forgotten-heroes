Eartha Kitt: The Unforgettable Purr of a Legend

Singer, Actress, Activist - The Most Exciting Woman in the World

A Timeless Beacon of Resilience and Talent

In an era where stars flicker and fade, Eartha Kitt stands as a timeless beacon of resilience, talent, and unapologetic flair. Born into abject poverty on January 17, 1927, in North, South Carolina, Kitt rose to become a multifaceted icon: singer, actress, dancer, activist, and the sultry voice behind unforgettable characters.

From Cotton Fields to Center Stage

Kitt's early years were marred by hardship that could have crushed lesser spirits. Of mixed Cherokee, African, and possibly European descent, she was born Eartha Mae Keith to a mother who worked on a cotton plantation. Abandoned and abused, Kitt endured extreme poverty, scavenging wild onions and greens for sustenance.

Sent to live with relatives in Harlem at age eight, she found solace in the arts, attending the High School of Performing Arts. By 16, she joined the Katherine Dunham Company, touring as a dancer and honing her multilingual singing prowess. Speaking four languages and performing in 11, Kitt's global appeal was immediate. Her cabaret acts in Paris and London in the 1940s captivated audiences with a voice that purred like velvet—sultry, sophisticated, and utterly unique.

The Voice That Captivated the World

Her music career exploded in the 1950s. Hits like "C'est si bon," "Uska Dara," and the cheeky "Santa Baby" (1953) showcased her torch song mastery, blending jazz, pop, and international flavors. "Santa Baby" alone became a cultural phenomenon, re-recorded by countless artists but never matching Kitt's playful seduction.

Eartha's Musical Milestones

  • Six U.S. Top 30 entries in the 1950s
  • UK Top 10 with "Under the Bridges of Paris"
  • "Santa Baby" (1953) became a holiday classic

She scored six U.S. Top 30 entries and a UK Top 10 with "Under the Bridges of Paris." Even in later years, tracks like "Where Is My Man" (1983), her first gold record, proved her enduring relevance, hitting dance charts worldwide. Kitt's discography wasn't just entertainment; it was a statement of empowerment, often laced with wit and sensuality that challenged societal norms.

Electric On Screen and Stage

On screen and stage, Kitt was electric. Broadway roles in New Faces of 1952 and Timbuktu! (1978) earned her Tony nominations, while films like Anna Lucasta (1958) highlighted her dramatic range. But it's her 1967 stint as Catwoman in the Batman TV series that immortalized her for many.

"I didn't play Catwoman; I was Catwoman." – Eartha Kitt

As the first Black actress in the role, Kitt infused the character with feline grace and cunning allure, purring lines that became pop culture gold. Her performance influenced generations, from Halle Berry to Zoë Kravitz, making her a trailblazer in representation.

Voice acting revived her in the 2000s, with Yzma in The Emperor's New Groove (2000) earning her two Daytime Emmys and Annie Awards. Her gravelly timbre brought wicked humor to the villain, delighting new audiences. Kitt's accolades piled up: Grammy nominations, a Hollywood Walk of Fame star (1960), and a posthumous Emmy in 2010.

The Activist Who Risked Everything

Yet, her artistry was matched by activism that nearly derailed her career. In 1968, at a White House luncheon, Kitt confronted First Lady Lady Bird Johnson on the Vietnam War: "You send the best of this country off to be shot and maimed. No wonder the kids rebel."

The backlash was swift; labeled a "sadistic nymphomaniac" in a CIA dossier, she was blacklisted in the U.S., forcing her abroad. But Kitt persisted, advocating for civil rights, youth programs like her Kittsville Youth Foundation, and LGBT equality. She saw parallels between racial and sexual discrimination, championing same-sex marriage as a civil right long before it was mainstream.

Eartha's Activism

  • Confronted Lady Bird Johnson about Vietnam War in 1968
  • Blacklisted in the U.S. for her political views
  • Founded Kittsville Youth Foundation
  • Early champion of LGBT rights and same-sex marriage

Personal Life and Legacy

Personally, Kitt married once, to John William McDonald, with whom she had daughter Kitt McDonald Shapiro. She authored three autobiographies, revealing her vulnerabilities. Diagnosed with colon cancer, she passed on Christmas Day 2008 at 81, still performing until near the end.

"Eartha Kitt's legacy is one of subversion and strength. She transcended poverty to become a symbol of feminism, sex positivity, and resilience."

Eartha Kitt's legacy is one of subversion and strength. She transcended poverty to become a symbol of feminism, sex positivity, and resilience. In a world quick to forget, Kitt reminds us of the power of authenticity. South Carolina honors her birthday as Eartha Kitt Day, but globally, her influence endures in music, media, and movements. She wasn't just exciting—she was essential. For her purr that echoed defiance and delight, Eartha Kitt must never be forgotten.

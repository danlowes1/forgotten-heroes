Major Allison Digby Tatham-Warter - The Eccentric Hero of Arnhem

Military History 1917-1993

Major Allison Digby Tatham-Warter (21 May 1917 - 21 March 1993) was a British Army officer who served with distinction during the Second World War, particularly during the Battle of Arnhem.

He is best remembered for his extraordinary leadership, eccentric behavior, and remarkable courage during Operation Market Garden. Tatham-Warter became legendary for leading his men while carrying an umbrella into battle, claiming he could never forget the password if he had it with him, and famously using it to disable a German armored vehicle.

Early Life and Military Career

Digby Tatham-Warter was educated at Wellington College and the Royal Military College, Sandhurst. He was commissioned into the British Army and joined the Oxfordshire and Buckinghamshire Light Infantry. When the 2nd Battalion was converted to airlanding infantry and became part of the 1st Airlanding Brigade of the 1st Airborne Division, Tatham-Warter was given command of 'A' Company.

"I'm not going to remember some damn silly word every day. I'll just say 'Bill' and you'll know it's me."
- Digby Tatham-Warter on why he used an umbrella as identification

Prior to Operation Market Garden, Tatham-Warter expressed concern about the radio equipment's reliability. True to his predictions, the radios failed almost immediately upon landing, making the umbrella an unexpectedly effective communication tool as he could be easily identified by his men.

Heroism at Arnhem

During the Battle of Arnhem in September 1944, Tatham-Warter's leadership became legendary. He led his company in the capture of the northern end of the Arnhem road bridge and defended it against overwhelming German forces for three days and nights.

His most famous moment came when he used his rolled-up umbrella to disable a German armored vehicle by thrusting it through the viewing slit and incapacitating the driver. He also led a bayonet charge while wearing a bowler hat and carrying his trademark umbrella.

When the paras' senior medical officer expressed concern about the number of stray bullets, Tatham-Warter opened his umbrella and remarked, "Don't worry about the bullets, I've got an umbrella."

Capture and Escape

After the British forces were overwhelmed at Arnhem, Tatham-Warter was captured but escaped almost immediately by feigning injury and slipping away from his captors. He then organized an escape route for over 100 Allied soldiers, helping them cross the Rhine to safety.

He worked with the Dutch Resistance, often moving through German checkpoints disguised as a deaf and dumb Dutch peasant. His knowledge of the countryside and ability to blend in allowed him to continue his resistance activities for several months before finally making his way back to Allied lines.

Later Life and Legacy

After the war, Tatham-Warter moved to Kenya where he became a successful farmer and conservationist. He was involved in the creation of the Aberdare National Park and helped establish the Kenya Regiment.

His character was portrayed in the 1977 film "A Bridge Too Far" by Christopher Good, who captured his eccentric bravery and leadership. Tatham-Warter's umbrella became a symbol of British eccentricity and courage under fire.

He died in Kenya in 1993, but his legend lives on as one of the most colorful and courageous characters of the Second World War, embodying the spirit of improvisation and unwavering leadership in the face of overwhelming odds.

The Lost German Girl - A Snapshot of 1945

World War II, May 8, 1945

The "Lost German Girl" is a young woman captured in a haunting 55-second color film segment by U.S. Army Captain Oren W. Haglund on May 8, 1945, near Rokycany, Czechoslovakia (modern-day Czech Republic). Filmed amid the chaos of Nazi Germany's surrender, she appears in a column of defeated German POWs, her face swollen and bruised, clutching her hands in a silent plea for mercy.

Shot on 16mm Kodachrome, the footage shows her walking alone among Wehrmacht and SS prisoners, dressed in civilian clothes with no clear military insignia. Her injuries suggest a recent beating, likely by Czech partisans or Soviet forces targeting suspected collaborators in the war’s final days. Despite extensive research, her identity remains unknown, though myths like the debunked "Lore Bauer" story persist.

A Moment in the Chaos of Surrender

Filmed on a dusty road between Rokycany and Pilsen, the clip captures the aftermath of the Prague Offensive (May 6–11, 1945), as thousands of Germans fled the advancing Red Army to surrender to U.S. forces. The woman, labeled "SS girl" in Haglund’s notes, likely a misidentification, was possibly a civilian or low-level auxiliary caught in the Sudetenland’s violent reprisals. Her faint smile and clasped hands amid a defeated column symbolize the war’s human toll.

SS Girl—badly beaten up. VCS on face as she asks for mercy.—panning down to trousers.
— Captain Oren W. Haglund, shot slate notes, May 8, 1945

The Enduring Mystery

Since surfacing in the 1974 BBC series The World at War, the footage has sparked global fascination. Online forums, including a discussion on Axis History Forum active since November 2008, have debated her identity—from possible Luftwaffe auxiliaries to Sudeten German refugees—yet no definitive evidence has emerged. The mystery continues to captivate historians and internet sleuths alike.

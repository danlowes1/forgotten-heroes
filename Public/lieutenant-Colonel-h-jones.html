H. Jones: The Paratrooper's Charge That Echoed Across the South Atlantic

The Mad Major Who Became a Falklands Legend

The Hero of Goose Green

In the chill dawn of May 28, 1982, as peaty mists clung to the hills of East Falkland like a shroud, a lone figure in camouflage rose against a hail of Argentine bullets. Lieutenant Colonel Herbert "H" Jones, commanding officer of 2nd Battalion, The Parachute Regiment, wasn't content to direct from afar—he led the assault on Darwin Hill himself, bayonet fixed, heart ablaze.

Killed in that reckless, redemptive charge, Jones didn't just fall; he forged a legend that shattered the enemy's resolve and lit the path to British victory in the Falklands War. At 42, this Eton-educated everyman became the conflict's Victoria Cross icon, a reminder that one man's fury can rewrite the tide of history. In an age of drones and distant wars, H. Jones demands we remember: courage is the blade that cuts through the fog of fear.

From Boarding School Battlefields to Airborne Brotherhood

Born on May 14, 1940, in London's leafy Putney to an American artist father and a Welsh nurse mother, young Herbert Jones—H to all who knew him—grew up in a world of privilege laced with grit. The eldest of three brothers, he thrived at Eton College, where his lanky frame and sharp wit masked a restless soul itching for action.

Commissioned into the Devonshire and Dorset Regiment in 1960, Jones's trajectory was meteoric: by 1964, he'd traded regimental ties for the red beret of the Parachute Regiment, drawn to its creed of audacity and sacrifice. Nicknamed "The Mad Major" for his frontline flair—leading patrols in Northern Ireland, storming IRA strongholds in Belfast—he embodied the Paras' ethos: "Utrinque Paratus," ready for anything.

"We're going to win this." – H. Jones to his men aboard SS Canberra

By 1982, a lieutenant colonel at the peak of his powers, Jones was holidaying in France when Argentina's junta seized the Falklands on April 2, igniting a crisis that tested Britain's mettle. Rushing back, he took command of 2 PARA, a 650-strong force of elite sky soldiers airlifted 8,000 miles south aboard the requisitioned liner SS Canberra. As Task Force vessels sliced through the Atlantic, Jones drilled his men relentlessly on the decks, his voice a clarion call amid the gales. Little did they know, Goose Green—a windswept isthmus settlement held by 1,200 Argentines—would be their crucible.

The Fury at Goose Green: A Solo Symphony of Steel

The Battle of Goose Green erupted on May 27-28, a brutal 15-hour slog through burned-out gorse and machine-gun nests. Outnumbered and outgunned, 2 PARA faced a dug-in foe atop Darwin Hill, their advance stalled by withering fire that pinned platoons in the mud. Frustration mounted; radios crackled with pleas for artillery that never came soon enough.

Then, in a move straight from a Homeric epic, Jones seized the moment. Spotting a cluster of enemy trenches raking his left flank—overlooked by scouts—he grabbed two corporals, fixed his bayonet, and charged alone into the storm.

Bullets zipped like hornets as he bounded 30 yards uphill, grenades bursting in his wake. Reaching the first trench, he unleashed a hail of fire, slaying the gunners inside. Pressing on, he vaulted into the second, only to be met by a point-blank burst from a concealed Argentine. Fatally wounded in the chest and leg, Jones crumpled but not before his wild valor rippled outward.

"The CO's going for it!" echoed through the ranks, igniting a frenzy. Paras surged forward, overrunning the positions in a tide of red berets and righteous rage. By dawn, Goose Green was theirs: 1,200 Argentines surrendered, 112 civilians freed from internment, and the road to Stanley cracked open. Jones's body was recovered at first light, his SLR rifle still clutched tight.

"His actions... completely undermined their will to fight... setting the tone for the subsequent land victory." – Victoria Cross Citation

The citation for his posthumous VC captured the alchemy. Buried with full honors at Blue Beach War Cemetery on San Carlos, overlooking the invasion beaches, Jones's grave whispers of empires reclaimed not by machines, but by men who dared the divine.

An Unfading Flame: Why H. Jones Must Endure

H. Jones's shadow looms large over modern Britain—a street in Stanley bears his name, a plaque at Eton gleams in his memory, and his story fuels films like *Zulu* echoes in *Tumbledown*. Yet beyond accolades, he's the antidote to apathy: in a world of calculated risks, he reminds us that leadership isn't memos and maps, but blood and belief.

His son, Hugh, carries the torch as a colonel himself, while veterans toast "The Enigma" at reunions, his eccentricities—collecting antique swords, quoting Shakespeare amid shellfire—humanizing the myth.

Raise a glass to the Mad Major—may his fury forever fuel the free.
